Geopolitics

Geopolitics is often used in a way that is synonymous to international relations, as both study the global phenomenon of war, trade and diplomacy. The distinguishing factor the deeper emphasis geopolitics places on the geographic factors at play, such as climate, topography, demography, and natural resource abundance.

The first heuristic at play is one of constant war, of peoples grouped by nationality, ethnicity, religion, and culture, pursuing the acquisition of natural resources through means of military force and games of attrition in the context of conventional warfare. This is akin to the guiding principle of Imperialism, that might is right.

Any man-made product no matter how complex, resolves at a fundamental level to the supply of natural resources, then developed into complex products through industry and manufacturing. Natural resources include water, land, sunlight, vegetation, wildlife, and minerals. Natural resources are renewable if the rate of replenishment exceeds the rate of consumption, and non-renewable otherwise. Rare earth elements, heavy rare earth elements, and platinum group metals are some examples of commonly sought after mineral groups. Critical minerals are the minerals deemed most critical to economic stability and technological progress and varies from state to state.

A state can be profiled by the quantity and composition of its net imported and exported goods and services. A state can manipulate the economic profile of itself or its counterparts through the use of economic tools such as sanctions (trade bans, travel bans, asset freezes, etc.) and tariffs (import and export taxes) to strike a balance between protectionism and free trade. Excessive protectionist measures may preclude a state from the economic opportunity of free trade, and thus slow the rate of technological progress in the absence of foreign resources, as well as incentivise war. On the other hand, free trade de-incentivises the development of domestic industry through an increased reliance on industry abroad. Thus, a state becomes vulnerable to the strategy of its counterparts.

A blockade as a means of economic disruption is typically perceived as an act of war, given that the functioning of a state relies on the functioning of its supply chains. These supply chains along with infrastructure both domestic and abroad (intelligence, energy, military, command and control, etc.) all become primary targets during wartime. The prospect of nuclear warfare and the doctrine of MAD is often cited as an incentive for the pursuit of proxy warfare and regime change over direct conventional conflict. A higher occurrence of brinksmanship may be another product of the doctrine of MAD, where state actors attempt to produce credible threats to their counterparts whereby they risk the possibility of the worst-case outcome for both parties in order to have their counterparts capitulate to some desired outcome.

The description of states thus far as goal-seeking entities performing and acting rationally in accordance with some geopolitical calculus, is limited. A state is not a singular blank entity, it is an amalgam of state actors, multinationals, private and public enterprise, small and local business, and citizens, of which all have some form of communicative interface with each other, of which all have a unique dialogue. Diplomacy, negotiation, trade summits, direct calls with counterparts, are all forms of state to state communication. Televised addresses, political campaigns, protests, riots, are all forms of state to citizen and citizen to state communication. Briefings, policy recommendations, lobbying, political donations, are all forms of state to institution communication. These institutions may include state or state-affiliated institutions (think tanks, intelligence agencies, multinationals etc.) as well as private enterprise (industry representatives, lobbyists, etc.). Cultural and religious community events, festivities, as well as everyday commentary and discussion, are the forms citizens take to discuss and promote ideals with one another.
